A well-designed QR code balances aesthetics with functionality. While it's tempting to push creative boundaries, the primary goal remains unchanged: the code must scan reliably, every time, on every device.

This guide covers proven design principles that ensure your QR codes look professional while maintaining perfect scannability.

## The Foundation: Contrast

Contrast is the single most important design factor. Without adequate contrast between modules and background, scanners cannot read the code.

### The Rule

**Dark modules on light backgrounds.** Always.

This isn't arbitrary—it's how QR scanners work. The scanner interprets darker areas as "1" (data) and lighter areas as "0" (no data).

### Contrast Ratios

| Contrast Level | Example | Scannability |
|---------------|---------|--------------|
| High contrast | Black on white | Excellent |
| Good contrast | Dark navy on cream | Good |
| Moderate | Dark green on light gray | Acceptable |
| Poor | Medium gray on light gray | Unreliable |
| Inverted | White on black | Often fails |

<Callout type="warning" title="Important">
Avoid light-colored modules (light blue, yellow, pink) even on white backgrounds. These create insufficient contrast and cause scan failures, especially in bright lighting conditions.
</Callout>

### Testing Contrast

Before finalizing, test your color combination:

1. Print the QR code at intended size
2. Test with 3+ different phones
3. Try in various lighting conditions
4. Test from different angles
5. Have someone else test without instruction

## Color Selection

Color adds personality while maintaining scannability:

### Safe Color Choices

**Dark colors that work well:**
- Black (always safest)
- Dark navy blue
- Dark forest green
- Burgundy/maroon
- Dark purple
- Charcoal gray

**Background colors that work well:**
- White (always safest)
- Cream/off-white
- Light gray
- Light pastels (subtle)

### Colors to Avoid

**For modules:**
- Yellow (too light)
- Light blue
- Orange (marginal)
- Light green
- Any pastel shade

**For backgrounds:**
- Anything darker than light gray
- Busy patterns
- Gradients that cross modules
- Photography or images

### Brand Color Integration

You can incorporate brand colors thoughtfully:

1. **Use dark brand colors for modules** - Navy, forest green, burgundy
2. **Keep backgrounds neutral** - White or very light shades
3. **Save bright colors for surrounding design** - Not the QR code itself
4. **Test extensively** - Brand colors may look different printed

<Callout type="tip" title="Pro Tip">
If your brand uses light colors (yellow, light blue, orange), use them in the design around the QR code rather than in the code itself. A branded border or background image can convey brand identity without compromising scannability.
</Callout>

## Size Guidelines

QR codes must be large enough for cameras to resolve individual modules:

### Minimum Sizes

| Use Case | Minimum Size | Recommended |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Business cards | 0.6 inch (15mm) | 0.8 inch (20mm) |
| Flyers/brochures | 0.8 inch (20mm) | 1 inch (25mm) |
| Posters | 1.5 inch (40mm) | 2 inch (50mm) |
| Banners/signage | 3 inch (75mm) | 4+ inch (100mm+) |
| Billboard | 12+ inch (300mm) | Calculate by viewing distance |

### The Distance Rule

**Scanning distance ÷ 10 = minimum QR size**

For a poster viewed from 6 feet (72 inches) away:
- 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 inches minimum

This rule ensures the camera can capture enough detail to decode the code.

### Module Size Consideration

Each QR code has a version (1-40) that determines how many modules it contains:

| Version | Modules | Content Capacity | Required Resolution |
|---------|---------|------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | 21×21 | Simple URL | Lower |
| 5 | 37×37 | Medium text | Moderate |
| 10 | 57×57 | vCard | Higher |
| 20 | 97×97 | Long text | Much higher |

## Logo Integration

Adding logos is popular for branding, but requires care:

### Logo Size Limits

The logo covers part of the code, which means that data is lost. Error correction compensates, but only to a point:

| Error Correction | Max Logo Coverage |
|------------------|-------------------|
| L (7%) | Not recommended |
| M (15%) | ~10% safely |
| Q (25%) | ~15-20% safely |
| H (30%) | ~25% safely |

<Callout type="info" title="Did You Know?">
QRWolf automatically uses Level H error correction when you add a logo, ensuring reliable scanning even with significant logo coverage.
</Callout>

### Logo Best Practices

1. **Center the logo** - The center has the most redundant data
2. **Use simple logos** - Detailed logos don't show well at small sizes
3. **Add padding** - Small white border around logo improves scanning
4. **Test thoroughly** - Every device, multiple lighting conditions
5. **Keep it square** - Circular logos work too, but avoid wide rectangles

### Logo Don'ts

- **Don't cover finder patterns** - The three corner squares are essential
- **Don't exceed 25%** - Even with high error correction
- **Don't use transparent backgrounds** - Solid backgrounds behind logos
- **Don't add multiple logos** - One centered logo only

## Quiet Zone Requirements

The **quiet zone** is the white space border around the QR code:

### Minimum Requirements

- **Standard minimum** - 4 modules wide
- **Recommended** - 6+ modules for print
- **Digital displays** - 4 modules usually sufficient

### Why It Matters

The quiet zone helps scanners:

- Find the edge of the code
- Separate the code from surrounding content
- Establish the reading frame
- Prevent false pattern detection

### Common Mistakes

- Cropping too close to the code edge
- Placing text or images right against the code
- Using busy backgrounds that intrude on quiet zone
- Colored borders that reduce effective quiet zone

The three corner squares can be customized:

- Standard squares (most reliable)
- Rounded corners
- Circles within squares
- Simple color variations

<Callout type="warning" title="Important">
Heavily stylized patterns may look distinctive but can cause scanning issues. Always test thoroughly before committing to printed materials.
</Callout>

## Print Considerations

### Resolution Requirements

| Print Method | Minimum DPI | Recommended DPI |
|--------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Home inkjet | 150 DPI | 300 DPI |
| Commercial print | 300 DPI | 600 DPI |
| Large format | 100 DPI | 150 DPI |

### File Formats

- **SVG** - Best for print (infinitely scalable, no quality loss)
- **PNG (high-res)** - Good for most uses at 300+ DPI
- **PDF** - Excellent for professional printing
- **JPEG** - Avoid (compression artifacts affect scanning)

### Material Considerations

| Material | Considerations |
|----------|----------------|
| Glossy paper | Watch for glare |
| Matte paper | Best for scanning |
| Plastic/vinyl | Ensure no warping |
| Fabric | Requires larger size |
| Metal | Use matte finish |
| Glass | Place behind, not on |

## Digital Display Best Practices

### Screen Brightness

- Keep brightness above 50%
- Disable auto-dim features
- Account for ambient lighting
- Test at various brightness levels

### Motion and Animation

- QR codes must remain static
- No animation or movement
- No auto-scrolling content
- Brief display duration (5+ seconds)

### Screen Protectors

- Matte protectors may reduce scanning success
- Clean screens scan better
- Test with protector in place

## Accessibility Considerations

### Context and Instructions

- Clearly label what scanning does
- Provide text alternative (URL, phone number)
- Include call-to-action near code
- Don't rely solely on QR for critical information

### Visual Considerations

- Ensure adequate size for visibility
- Consider color blindness (contrast matters, not specific colors)
- Place at accessible heights
- Provide alternative access methods

## Common Design Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Inverting Colors
Light modules on dark backgrounds fail because scanners expect dark-on-light.

### Mistake 2: Insufficient Contrast
"It looks fine on screen" doesn't mean it will scan. Print and test.

### Mistake 3: Too Small
The most common failure. When in doubt, go larger.

### Mistake 4: Logo Too Large
Covering more than 25% risks scan failures.

### Mistake 5: Busy Backgrounds
Patterns, images, or gradients behind QR codes cause detection failures.

### Mistake 6: No Quiet Zone
Cutting the code too close or placing it against busy elements.

## Design Checklist

Before finalizing any QR code design:

- [ ] Dark modules on light background
- [ ] Contrast ratio tested on paper
- [ ] Adequate size for viewing distance
- [ ] Logo covers less than 25% (if used)
- [ ] Level H error correction (if logo used)
- [ ] Quiet zone of 4+ modules
- [ ] Tested on 3+ different devices
- [ ] Tested in multiple lighting conditions
- [ ] Print resolution adequate (300+ DPI)
- [ ] Alternative access method provided

## Conclusion

Great QR code design is invisible—users don't notice the code itself, just the seamless scan experience. By following these best practices, you ensure every scan succeeds while maintaining the professional appearance your brand deserves.

Remember: when aesthetics and functionality conflict, functionality must win. A beautiful QR code that doesn't scan is worthless, while a plain black-and-white code that scans every time serves its purpose perfectly.
