## What is Error Correction?

**Error correction** is the technology that makes QR codes remarkably resilient. Unlike traditional barcodes that fail with even minor damage, QR codes can be partially obscured, scratched, or covered and still scan successfully.

This isn't magic—it's mathematics. QR codes use a sophisticated algorithm called **Reed-Solomon error correction** that adds redundant data to every code, allowing scanners to reconstruct missing or corrupted information.

## The Four Error Correction Levels

QR codes offer four levels of error correction, each providing a different balance between data capacity and damage resistance:

| Level | Recovery Capacity | Best For |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| **L** (Low) | ~7% damage | Clean environments, maximum data |
| **M** (Medium) | ~15% damage | General purpose use |
| **Q** (Quartile) | ~25% damage | Industrial environments |
| **H** (High) | ~30% damage | Heavy wear, logo overlays |

<Callout type="info" title="Did You Know?">
The letters L, M, Q, and H stand for Low, Medium, Quartile, and High—not quality grades, but the percentage of data that can be restored if damaged.
</Callout>

## How Reed-Solomon Works

The Reed-Solomon algorithm, invented in 1960, works by treating your data as a mathematical polynomial and adding calculated "check" values:

1. **Data encoding** - Your content (URL, text, etc.) is converted to numbers
2. **Polynomial creation** - These numbers form a mathematical equation
3. **Error codewords** - Additional values are calculated and appended
4. **Pattern generation** - Everything is arranged in the QR grid pattern

When a scanner reads the code:

1. It detects the data and error codewords
2. Checks for inconsistencies using the polynomial math
3. If errors exist, calculates what the original values should be
4. Reconstructs the correct data

### A Simple Analogy

Imagine writing a message where every third word is a summary of the previous two. If someone smudges a word, you could figure out what it said from context. Reed-Solomon does this mathematically, allowing precise reconstruction.

## Choosing the Right Level

### Level L (7% Recovery)

**Use when:**
- QR codes will be displayed on screens
- Environment is clean and controlled
- Maximum data capacity is needed
- Code will be replaced frequently

**Example:** Digital displays, websites, app interfaces

### Level M (15% Recovery)

**Use when:**
- General business applications
- Indoor printed materials
- Moderate handling expected
- Balance of capacity and resilience needed

**Example:** Business cards, flyers, product packaging

### Level Q (25% Recovery)

**Use when:**
- Outdoor or industrial environments
- Codes may get dirty or wet
- Longer lifespan required
- Some handling damage expected

**Example:** Warehouse labels, outdoor signage, construction sites

### Level H (30% Recovery)

**Use when:**
- Adding logos or designs to the center
- Harsh environmental conditions
- Maximum reliability required
- Data capacity is not a concern

**Example:** Marketing materials with branded QR codes, restaurant menus

<Callout type="tip" title="Pro Tip">
If you're adding a logo to your QR code, always use Level H error correction. The logo covers part of the code, and you need that extra redundancy for reliable scanning.
</Callout>

## Error Correction and Code Size

Higher error correction means more redundant data, which affects your QR code in two ways:

### 1. Physical Size

For the same content, higher error correction creates denser (or larger) codes:

| Content | Level L Size | Level H Size |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Short URL | 21×21 modules | 25×25 modules |
| vCard | 57×57 modules | 73×73 modules |
| Long text | 101×101 modules | 129×129 modules |

### 2. Data Capacity

Higher error correction reduces how much data you can store:

| Level | Numeric Capacity | Alphanumeric | Binary |
|-------|------------------|--------------|--------|
| L | 7,089 chars | 4,296 chars | 2,953 bytes |
| M | 5,596 chars | 3,391 chars | 2,331 bytes |
| Q | 3,993 chars | 2,420 chars | 1,663 bytes |
| H | 3,057 chars | 1,852 chars | 1,273 bytes |

## Real-World Error Correction

### The Logo Test

When you add a logo to a QR code, you're deliberately "damaging" it. Here's what happens:

- A centered logo covering 10% of the code needs Level M minimum
- A logo covering 15-20% requires Level Q
- A logo covering 25%+ requires Level H
- Beyond 30% coverage, even Level H can't save you

### Environmental Damage

Common damage scenarios and recommended levels:

| Scenario | Minimum Level |
|----------|---------------|
| Coffee shop table tents | M |
| Outdoor event banners | Q |
| Factory floor labels | Q or H |
| Restaurant menus (heavy use) | H |
| Digital screens | L |

## Testing Your QR Codes

Always test QR codes under realistic conditions:

1. **Print at actual size** - Don't rely on screen tests alone
2. **Test multiple devices** - Both iPhone and Android
3. **Simulate damage** - Cover portions with your finger
4. **Check from distance** - Ensure adequate scanning range
5. **Try different lighting** - Bright sun, dim indoor, etc.

<Callout type="warning" title="Important">
A QR code that barely scans in perfect conditions will fail in real-world use. Always build in a safety margin by using one level higher than minimum required.
</Callout>

## Best Practices Summary

1. **Default to Level M** for most business applications
2. **Use Level H** when adding logos or for harsh environments
3. **Use Level L** only for digital displays or when data capacity is critical
4. **Test before printing** - Always verify real-world performance
5. **Consider dynamic codes** - They're naturally smaller and more resilient
