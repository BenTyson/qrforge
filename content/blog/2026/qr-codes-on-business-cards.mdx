Business cards have a fundamental problem: they rely on someone else taking action. Hand over a card, and the recipient must later decide to type your name into their phone, search for your email, or look you up on LinkedIn. Every step in that process is an opportunity for them to get distracted, lose the card, or simply forget. A QR code short-circuits this entire sequence—one scan captures your information instantly, while both of you are still standing there.

Adding a QR code to your business card isn't complicated, but doing it well requires thinking through what you're linking to, where the code goes, and how it integrates with your overall card design.

## What Should Your QR Code Link To?

The destination matters more than the code itself. Several options serve different networking goals.

**vCard (contact card)** remains the most practical choice for most professionals. A [vCard QR code](/blog/vcard-qr-codes-digital-business-cards) encodes your name, phone, email, job title, company, and address directly. When scanned, the recipient's phone offers to save you as a contact immediately—no typing, no searching, no room for transcription errors.

The advantage of vCard is completeness. Every piece of contact information from your physical card transfers digitally. The disadvantage is that vCard data is static; if your phone number changes, your printed cards point to outdated information.

**LinkedIn profile** links make sense for professionals where LinkedIn is the primary relationship channel. Sales professionals, recruiters, consultants, and executives often prefer driving connections to LinkedIn where the relationship can continue beyond the initial exchange. See our guide on [creating LinkedIn QR codes](/blog/qr-code-for-linkedin) for details.

**Personal or company website** works when you want to showcase work rather than just exchange contact details. Designers, photographers, developers, and consultants might prefer directing people to portfolios rather than contact forms.

**Digital business card landing page** offers a hybrid approach. Services like [QRWolf's Links feature](/learn/links-qr-code) let you create a landing page with multiple contact methods, social profiles, and links—essentially a rich digital business card that goes far beyond what printed cards can contain.

## Placement and Sizing

Business card real estate is limited. Standard US cards (3.5" × 2") and European cards (85mm × 55mm) offer perhaps 7 square inches to convey professional identity. The QR code needs to work within that constraint without overwhelming other content.

**Back of the card** is the most common placement. The front carries essential information—name, title, company, primary contact details. The back can be devoted entirely to the QR code with supporting text like "Scan to save contact." This keeps the front clean while providing a prominent, scannable code.

**Front corner placement** works if you want everything on one side. A code about 0.75" to 1" square fits comfortably in a corner without competing with name and title text. This approach keeps all content visible at a glance but requires careful design to avoid crowding.

**Minimum size** for reliable scanning is roughly 0.6" (15mm) square. Smaller codes risk scan failures, especially on phones with less capable cameras or in suboptimal lighting. For business cards, 0.75" to 1" provides a comfortable margin above the minimum.

The code needs adequate quiet zone—the white space surrounding it. Most generators add this automatically, but ensure your design doesn't crowd other elements against the code boundary. A frame or border can help define the code's space while adding visual appeal.

## Design Integration

A QR code shouldn't look like an afterthought stuck onto an otherwise-designed card. Thoughtful integration makes the code feel intentional.

**Color coordination** aligns the code with your card's palette. If your card uses navy and white, consider a navy QR code rather than default black. The code should contrast sufficiently with the background (see our [color contrast guide](/learn/qr-code-colors-contrast)), but within that constraint, brand colors typically work.

**Logo embedding** places your logo or photo in the code's center. This is possible due to QR codes' error correction—up to 30% of the code can be obscured while remaining scannable. QRWolf's [Pro plan](/plans) supports logo uploads for branded codes. Test thoroughly, as overly large logos can compromise scannability.

**Consistent styling** means the code matches your card's aesthetic. A minimalist card needs a clean code; an ornate card can accommodate more decorative framing around the code. The code shouldn't look like it came from a different design language.

**Call-to-action clarity** tells people what scanning will do. "Scan to save contact" or "Connect on LinkedIn" sets expectations. A mysterious code with no explanation will be ignored by cautious scanners.

## Vertical vs. Horizontal Cards

Card orientation affects optimal QR code placement.

**Horizontal (landscape) cards** typically place QR codes on the right side of the back, leaving room for additional text or branding on the left. On the front, bottom corners work well since name and title usually appear upper-left.

**Vertical (portrait) cards** often place codes at the bottom center of the back, with the call-to-action text above. Front placement on vertical cards is trickier due to limited width; back-only placement usually looks cleaner.

Non-standard card sizes and shapes require individual consideration. Oversized cards accommodate larger codes; mini cards may need to choose between QR code presence and scannability.

## Material Considerations

Business card materials affect QR code performance.

**Matte paper** scans reliably without glare issues. The non-reflective surface works well in various lighting conditions.

**Glossy or coated paper** can create glare that interferes with scanning. This doesn't make glossy cards impossible—people simply angle the card to avoid reflection—but it's worth testing your specific combination of finish, code size, and colors.

**Textured paper** with heavy grain or embossing can distort the code pattern. Test before committing to production, especially with smaller codes where texture has proportionally more impact.

**Metal, wood, and specialty materials** require careful testing. Metal cards with etched codes look impressive but may scan differently than printed codes. Some specialty materials work beautifully; others fail entirely.

**Dark backgrounds** are risky. QR codes on dark cards require inverted color schemes (light modules on dark background), which many scanners handle poorly. If your brand mandates dark cards, either add a white border around the code or accept reduced scan reliability.

<Callout type="warning" title="Print Quality Matters">
QR codes require precision. Low-quality printing can blur module edges or cause ink bleeding that makes codes unscannable. Order proofs from your printer and test actual samples before approving production runs.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

**Codes too small to scan.** Designers prioritizing aesthetics over function shrink codes until they're unreliable. The minimum works in perfect conditions; real conditions aren't perfect.

**Insufficient contrast.** A light gray code on white paper looks sophisticated but barely scans. Prioritize contrast over subtlety.

**No call-to-action.** A code without context doesn't get scanned. People need to know what they're getting.

**Linking to desktop-optimized content.** Business card scans happen on phones. If your QR code links to a website that's unusable on mobile, you've wasted everyone's time.

**Static codes with changeable information.** If you might change jobs, phone numbers, or platforms, static codes locking in today's information create future problems.

**Overcomplicated codes.** Long URLs create dense codes with tiny modules. Use short links or dynamic codes to keep complexity manageable at business card sizes.

## Testing Your Cards

Before printing hundreds of cards, test thoroughly.

Order a small proof batch first. Print quality varies, and what looks good on screen may not scan well when printed.

Test with multiple phones. Your iPhone might scan a code that older Android devices struggle with. Include diverse devices in your testing.

Test in various lighting. Office lighting, dim restaurants, outdoor sunlight—business cards get scanned everywhere.

Test at arm's length. People naturally hold phones at a comfortable distance when scanning. If they need to position the phone precisely to get a scan, the code's marginal.

Have others test. You know where the code is and what it does. Someone seeing the card fresh tests whether the design communicates effectively.
